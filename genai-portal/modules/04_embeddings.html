Module 04 · Retrieval track

Embeddings — turning meaning into math

An LLM can reason about text, but it can't search millions of documents to find what's relevant — that would blow past any context window. Embeddings solve this: they compress a piece of text into a vector of numbers so that similar meanings land near each other in space. Distance becomes a proxy for relevance, and suddenly semantic search, RAG, clustering, and dedup are all just geometry.

⏱ Study + practice · 8–12 h Difficulty · Intermediate Prereq · Modules 01–02

1 · Executive summary

An embedding is a fixed-length vector (e.g. 384, 768, or 1536 numbers) produced by a model that has learned to map text into a space where semantic similarity equals geometric closeness. "How do I reset my password?" and "I forgot my login credentials" produce nearby vectors even though they share almost no words. That property is the foundation of every retrieval system in this course.

❓

Why it exists

Keyword search misses meaning ("car" ≠ "automobile"). Embeddings capture semantics so search works on intent, not exact strings.

🔧

Problem it solves

"Find the few relevant passages out of millions, fast." Convert everything to vectors once, then compare by distance.

🌐

Where it fits

The first half of RAG. Embeddings feed the vector DB (Module 05) and power retrieval (Module 06).

🔑
The one idea to remember

An embedding is a coordinate for meaning. Text → vector once; then "relevant" becomes "close." Cosine similarity is the ruler you measure closeness with.

2 · First-principles breakdown

What — a map where neighbors mean similar things

Picture a giant map. Every sentence gets a pin. The model places pins so that sentences about refunds cluster in one region, shipping in another, poetry far away. The map has hundreds of dimensions (not two), but the intuition holds: nearby pins = related meaning. To find passages relevant to a question, you embed the question, drop its pin, and grab the nearest neighbors.

🧭
The classic analogy

The famous result king − man + woman ≈ queen shows directions in the space carry meaning. Modern sentence embeddings extend this from single words to whole passages — the geometry encodes semantics.

Why — distance is cheap, understanding is expensive

Comparing two vectors with cosine similarity is a single dot product — microseconds. Asking an LLM "are these two paragraphs about the same thing?" costs a full forward pass and dollars. Embeddings move the expensive "understanding" step to a one-time encode, then let cheap geometry do the matching at query time across millions of items.

How — from transformer to a single vector

An embedding model is usually a transformer encoder (Module 02, bidirectional). It reads the text, produces a contextual vector per token, then pools them into one vector (mean-pooling or a special [CLS] token). The model is trained with contrastive learning: pull similar pairs together, push dissimilar pairs apart.

4 · Deep technical explanation

Measuring similarity

MetricFormulaNotes
Cosine similarity(A·B)/(‖A‖‖B‖)Angle between vectors; ignores magnitude. The default for text. Range −1…1.
Dot productA·BCosine × magnitudes. Equals cosine when vectors are normalized — and most are.
Euclidean (L2)‖A − B‖Straight-line distance. Equivalent ranking to cosine for normalized vectors.
✅

Normalize, then use dot product. If you L2-normalize every vector to unit length, dot product == cosine similarity but is faster and is what vector DBs optimize. Most modern embedding models already output normalized vectors — confirm before mixing metrics.

Dense vs. sparse embeddings

Dense (semantic)

Few hundred–few thousand floats, every dim is "active." Captures meaning/synonyms. Misses exact keywords, codes, and rare proper nouns. This module's focus.

Sparse (lexical)

Vocabulary-sized, mostly zeros (BM25, SPLADE). Captures exact terms, IDs, jargon. Misses paraphrase. We combine both in hybrid search (Module 07).

Choosing an embedding model

ConsiderationWhat to weigh
Dimensionality384 (fast, light) vs 768/1024 (richer) vs 1536+ (best, heavier storage & compute). More dims = more storage and slower search.
Domain fitGeneral (all-MiniLM, bge, e5) vs domain-tuned (code, legal, biomedical). Match the model to your data.
Max sequence lengthInputs beyond the limit are truncated — your tail text silently vanishes. Drives your chunk size.
Asymmetric vs symmetricSome models (e5, bge) want prefixes like "query:" / "passage:". Using them wrong tanks recall.
Local vs hostedRun open models (sentence-transformers, bge, nomic) via Ollama for privacy; hosted for convenience/quality.

Why chunk granularity is an embedding decision

A single vector must summarize an entire input. Embed a whole 20-page PDF into one vector and it becomes a mushy average — useless for retrieval. Embed each sentence and you lose surrounding context. The sweet spot (typically a few hundred tokens per chunk, with overlap) is a tradeoff you tune in Module 06; the constraint originates here, in how embeddings compress.

Tradeoffs & limitations

Strengths

  • Meaning-based matching
  • One-time encode, cheap query-time search
  • Language- and format-agnostic

Limits

  • Weak on exact terms/IDs (→ hybrid)
  • "Curse of dimensionality" — everything looks moderately similar at scale
  • Model-specific: never mix vectors from different models
  • Encodes training biases

6 · Practical Python

Minimal — embed text & compare meanings

minimal_embed.py
from sentence_transformers import SentenceTransformer, util

model = SentenceTransformer("all-MiniLM-L6-v2")   # 384-d, fast, local

a = model.encode("How do I reset my password?", normalize_embeddings=True)
b = model.encode("I forgot my login credentials", normalize_embeddings=True)
c = model.encode("What's the weather tomorrow?", normalize_embeddings=True)

print("a↔b", util.cos_sim(a, b).item())   # ~0.6  (related)
print("a↔c", util.cos_sim(a, c).item())   # ~0.1  (unrelated)

Intermediate — a tiny semantic search index (NumPy)

mini_search.py
import numpy as np
from sentence_transformers import SentenceTransformer

model = SentenceTransformer("all-MiniLM-L6-v2")
docs = [
    "Reset your password from the account settings page.",
    "Our refund policy allows returns within 30 days.",
    "Two-factor authentication adds a second login step.",
    "Shipping is free on orders over $50.",
]
# Index once: normalized so dot product == cosine
doc_vecs = model.encode(docs, normalize_embeddings=True)

def search(query, k=2):
    q = model.encode(query, normalize_embeddings=True)
    scores = doc_vecs @ q                  # cosine via dot product
    top = np.argsort(-scores)[:k]
    return [(docs[i], round(float(scores[i]), 3)) for i in top]

print(search("I can't log in"))
# [('Reset your password...', 0.52), ('Two-factor authentication...', 0.41)]

Production — a batched, cached, fault-tolerant embedder

embedder.py
import hashlib, logging
from functools import lru_cache
from sentence_transformers import SentenceTransformer

log = logging.getLogger("embedder")

class Embedder:
    """Batched embedding with caching + model-version tagging."""
    def __init__(self, model_name="BAAI/bge-small-en-v1.5", batch_size=64):
        self.name = model_name
        self.model = SentenceTransformer(model_name)
        self.batch_size = batch_size

    def version(self) -> str:
        # Tag stored vectors with model id — never mix models in one index!
        return self.name

    def embed_docs(self, texts: list[str]):
        # bge wants a query prefix at search time, not for passages
        return self.model.encode(texts, batch_size=self.batch_size,
                                 normalize_embeddings=True,
                                 show_progress_bar=False)

    @lru_cache(maxsize=10_000)
    def embed_query(self, text: str):
        prefixed = f"query: {text}"            # asymmetric model convention
        return self.model.encode(prefixed, normalize_embeddings=True)

    def key(self, text: str) -> str:
        return hashlib.sha256((self.name + text).encode()).hexdigest()
⚠️

Tag every stored vector with its model version. When you upgrade embedding models, the old vectors are incompatible — you must re-embed the whole corpus. Mixing models in one index silently destroys retrieval quality.

7 · Ollama implementation

Embeddings can run fully locally too — keep your documents private end-to-end.

bash
# Pull a dedicated local embedding model
ollama pull nomic-embed-text     # 768-d, strong general embeddings
ollama pull mxbai-embed-large    # 1024-d, higher quality
ollama pull bge-m3               # multilingual + dense/sparse hybrid
local_embed.py
import ollama, numpy as np

def embed(text, model="nomic-embed-text"):
    v = ollama.embeddings(model=model, prompt=text)["embedding"]
    v = np.array(v)
    return v / np.linalg.norm(v)          # normalize → cosine via dot

q   = embed("local embedding models")
doc = embed("running sentence encoders on your own GPU")
print("cosine:", float(q @ doc))
🔒

Local embedding + local generation (Module 03) = a RAG pipeline where no text ever leaves your machine. This is the privacy backbone of the on-prem capstones.

8 · Industry perspective

🚀 Startups

Start with a small open model (all-MiniLM/bge-small) for cost and speed, hosted embeddings if convenience wins. Re-embedding cost is small early — they iterate on chunking before optimizing the model.

🏢 Enterprises

Standardize on one embedding model across teams (so vectors are comparable), version it explicitly, and plan re-embedding pipelines as a first-class job. Often fine-tune embeddings on domain data for a measurable recall lift.

Beyond RAG: where embeddings show up

  • Deduplication & clustering — group near-identical tickets, articles, or logs.
  • Classification — nearest-centroid or kNN over embeddings, often beating bespoke classifiers with little data.
  • Recommendation — "more like this" via nearest neighbors.
  • Anomaly detection — outliers are vectors far from every cluster.
  • Semantic caching — return a cached LLM answer if a new query is embeddings-close to a past one (cost saver, Module 14).

Common production challenges: embedding-model upgrades forcing full re-index, latency of embedding at query time (cache it), inconsistent normalization across services, and the query/passage prefix gotcha silently halving recall.

9 · Top mistakes engineers make

❌ Mixing models in one index

Vectors from different models live in different spaces — distances are meaningless. One index, one model, one version.

❌ Skipping normalization (then using dot product)

Unnormalized dot products rank by magnitude, not meaning. Normalize, or use cosine explicitly.

❌ Ignoring query/passage prefixes

Asymmetric models (e5, bge) need "query:"/"passage:" prefixes. Omit them and recall craters.

❌ Embedding huge chunks

One vector can't represent 20 pages. Oversized chunks average out the signal. Chunk thoughtfully (Module 06).

❌ Relying on dense alone for IDs/codes

Exact tokens (SKUs, error codes, names) need lexical/sparse search. Use hybrid (Module 07).

❌ Re-embedding the query every request

For repeated/popular queries, cache the query vector — it's pure compute you can avoid.

10 · Interview preparation

Beginner  What is a text embedding? ›

A fixed-length numeric vector representing the meaning of a piece of text, produced by a model trained so that semantically similar texts get nearby vectors. It lets you measure relevance with geometric distance instead of keyword overlap.

Beginner  Why cosine similarity instead of Euclidean distance for text? ›

Cosine measures the angle between vectors, ignoring magnitude — and for text, direction (which concepts are present) matters more than length. With normalized vectors, cosine, dot product, and Euclidean give the same ranking, so people normalize and use the fast dot product.

Intermediate  Dense vs sparse embeddings — when do you need each? ›

Dense (semantic) embeddings match meaning and paraphrase but miss exact terms. Sparse/lexical (BM25, SPLADE) match exact keywords, codes, and rare proper nouns but miss synonyms. Use dense for conceptual queries, sparse for precise terms, and hybrid (combine both, then rerank) when you need both — covered in Module 07.

Intermediate  How does embedding dimensionality affect a system? ›

Higher dimensions can capture more nuance but cost more storage (linear in dims × number of vectors) and slightly slower similarity computation and index build. 384-d is fast/light; 768–1024 is a common quality balance; 1536+ for top quality. Choose based on recall needs vs. storage/latency budget; some models (Matryoshka) let you truncate dims gracefully.

Senior  Your retrieval quality dropped overnight. Embeddings are a suspect — how do you investigate? ›

Check for a silent model/version change (different model → incompatible space) or a normalization/prefix regression (someone dropped the query: prefix). Verify index and query use the same model + same preprocessing. Inspect a few queries' nearest neighbors manually. Run your retrieval eval set (recall@k, MRR) against a known-good baseline. If a model upgrade happened, you likely need a full re-embed; partial re-index leaves mixed spaces.

Staff · design  Design an embedding platform serving 50 teams over billions of documents. ›

Centralize a versioned embedding service (one or few approved models) so vectors are comparable and re-index jobs are coordinated. Components: a batched GPU embedding API with autoscaling; a feature/registry that records model id + version + normalization alongside every vector; an idempotent, resumable re-embedding pipeline (model upgrades = backfill jobs); a cache layer for hot query vectors and semantic caching; cost/latency dashboards per team. Governance: deprecation policy for models, eval gates before any model swap (recall@k regression check), and PII handling for inputs. Treat the embedding model like a database schema — versioned, migrated deliberately, never silently changed.

11 · Knowledge check

1 · Two sentences with no words in common can still have high embedding similarity. Why?
Embeddings encode semantics, not surface words. "Reset my password" and "forgot my login" share meaning, so their vectors are close despite different words.
2 · You normalize all vectors to unit length. Now dot product equals…
Cosine = (A·B)/(‖A‖‖B‖). If ‖A‖=‖B‖=1, the denominator is 1, so dot product == cosine — faster and what vector DBs optimize.
3 · Which task most needs sparse/lexical search alongside dense embeddings?
Dense embeddings blur exact tokens. SKUs, IDs, and error codes are precise strings that lexical/sparse search matches reliably — hence hybrid search.
4 · You upgrade your embedding model. What must you do?
Different models produce incompatible spaces. Mixing old and new vectors makes distances meaningless — you must re-embed everything and version the index.

12 · Active recall

Answer first, then reveal. Re-test tomorrow and in a week.

Q In one sentence, what does an embedding model do? ›
Maps text to a fixed-length vector such that semantically similar texts are close in the vector space.
Q Write the cosine similarity formula. ›
cos(A,B) = (A·B) / (‖A‖·‖B‖). For unit-normalized vectors it reduces to the dot product A·B.
Q Why must index-time and query-time use the same embedding model? ›
Different models produce vectors in different, incomparable spaces. Distances are only meaningful within one model's space, so both sides must match (and use the same normalization/prefixes).
Q What is the query/passage prefix gotcha? ›
Asymmetric models (e5, bge) are trained to prepend query: to queries and passage: to documents. Omitting or swapping them significantly degrades retrieval recall.
Q Name two non-RAG uses of embeddings. ›
Any two of: deduplication, clustering, classification (kNN/centroid), recommendation, anomaly detection, semantic caching.

Medium

🔴
Hard
