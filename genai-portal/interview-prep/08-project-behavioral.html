Project Deep-Dive & Behavioural Questions

Your project story should prove ownership: the problem, constraints, decisions, measurements, failures and what you would improve.

10 high-signal questionsFinal-round · senior signalAnswer aloud, then open
Interview answers become stronger when they connect model behavior to context, controls and measurable product outcomes.
🎯
How to practise this topic

Use specific evidence. Replace “we used” with your decision or contribution, name a metric, and explain one trade-off honestly.

10 questions
01Walk me through a GenAI project you built.projectstorytellingownership＋
Strong answer

I use a five-part structure: user problem and success metric; constraints and risk; architecture and why; my specific ownership; results, failures and next improvement. I draw the request flow and spend more time on decisions and measurements than on listing libraries.

30-second version

Problem, constraints, architecture, my ownership, measured outcome and lessons. Keep it causal and specific.

Likely follow-up: What was the hardest technical decision?
02Why did you choose that tech stack?stacktrade-offarchitecture＋
Strong answer

I map each choice to a requirement: FastAPI for typed async APIs, PostgreSQL for transactional truth, pgvector for moderate-scale retrieval with relational filters, a workflow engine for durable waits, and a hosted model for speed to market. I also say what alternatives I considered and the trigger for changing the decision.

30-second version

Tie every technology to a requirement and trade-off. Name the alternative and when you would switch.

Likely follow-up: Which stack choice would you change today?
03Describe a production failure and how you handled it.incidentreliabilitybehavioural＋
Strong answer

I explain impact, detection, immediate mitigation, root cause and prevention. For example, a document parser change reduced retrieval recall: we rolled back ingestion, reprocessed affected documents, added corpus-version checks and created regression cases. I avoid blame and show how observability shortened recovery.

30-second version

State the impact, contain it, find the root cause and turn it into a permanent control or test.

Likely follow-up: What did you personally do during the incident?
04How did you improve model or RAG quality?qualityevaluationrag＋
Strong answer

I first created a labeled error taxonomy and baseline. Then I changed one stage at a time—parsing, chunking, retrieval, reranking, prompt or model—and measured retrieval and answer metrics separately. I report the delta on critical slices, not only an average score.

30-second version

Baseline, classify errors, change one stage, measure by slice, and keep the regression set.

Likely follow-up: How did you know the improvement was real?
05How did you reduce latency or cost?latencycostoptimization＋
Strong answer

I show the trace breakdown and target the dominant stage. Typical changes include parallel tool calls, better retrieval to shrink context, smaller-model routing, response limits, caching and moving non-interactive work to queues. I include before/after p95 and cost per successful task, plus any quality trade-off.

30-second version

Measure the bottleneck, optimize it, and report p95, cost and quality before and after.

Likely follow-up: Which optimization had the greatest risk?
06Tell me about an architecture decision you owned.ownershiparchitectureleadership＋
Strong answer

I describe the decision boundary, options, data gathered, stakeholders, chosen approach and reversible checkpoints. For instance, I may choose a deterministic workflow with one bounded agent rather than a fully autonomous system because auditability and predictable latency mattered more than flexibility.

30-second version

Show the alternatives, evidence, decision and outcome. Senior ownership is informed trade-offs, not solo coding.

Likely follow-up: How did you get agreement?
07How do you explain a GenAI trade-off to a non-technical stakeholder?stakeholdercommunicationtrade-off＋
Strong answer

I frame it in product consequences. Instead of “model A has better benchmark scores,” I say it improves answer acceptance by an estimated amount but adds two seconds and higher cost. I offer options, uncertainty and a recommendation tied to user or business risk.

30-second version

Translate quality, latency, cost and risk into user and business outcomes, then recommend a reversible path.

Likely follow-up: How would you explain hallucination risk to leadership?
08What does your day-to-day work look like on a GenAI product?roleengineeringcollaboration＋
Strong answer

A strong answer includes data and failure analysis, evaluation design, Python or service implementation, prompt/RAG/agent iteration, code review, tracing, incident response and collaboration with product, security and domain experts. I emphasize that production GenAI is mostly system and data engineering around the model.

30-second version

I build and evaluate the whole system: data, retrieval, services, model behavior, observability and cross-functional controls.

Likely follow-up: Which part takes more time than candidates expect?
09How do you stay current without chasing every new framework?learningframeworksjudgment＋
Strong answer

I follow primary documentation and a small number of research or engineering sources, reproduce important claims on a compact benchmark, and update a decision log. I track durable capabilities—structured output, evaluation, retrieval, tool protocols and serving—not only framework names.

30-second version

Use primary sources, small experiments and a decision log. Follow capabilities and trade-offs rather than hype.

Likely follow-up: Name one recent change that altered your design choices.
10What would you do in your first 90 days in this role?90-daysleadershipplanning＋
Strong answer

First I would understand users, risk, data, architecture and current evaluation. Then I would reproduce the system locally, inspect traces and incidents, establish a baseline and deliver one contained improvement. By 90 days I would propose a prioritized roadmap for quality, reliability, security and cost with clear owners and metrics.

30-second version

Learn the product and risk, baseline the system, ship one measured improvement, then propose a metric-driven roadmap.

Likely follow-up: How would you choose the first improvement?
